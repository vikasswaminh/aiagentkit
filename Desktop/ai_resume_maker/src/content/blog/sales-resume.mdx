Sales is a results-driven profession, and your resume needs to reflect that from the first line. Hiring managers evaluating sales candidates look for one thing above all else: evidence that you can generate revenue. Unlike many other roles, sales performance is directly measurable, which means your resume should be packed with numbers, percentages, and dollar figures that prove your track record.

This guide walks through exactly what belongs on a sales resume, from summary statements to experience bullets that showcase real revenue impact. If you need a refresher on general resume principles, start with our [complete resume writing guide](/blog/how-to-write-a-resume) and return here for sales-specific strategies.

## What Hiring Managers Look For in Sales Resumes

Sales hiring managers read resumes the same way they evaluate pipeline reports: they scan for numbers first and context second. Here is what separates a strong sales resume from one that gets passed over.

**Quota attainment and revenue metrics.** This is the single most important element on a sales resume. Hiring managers want to see your quota, the percentage you achieved, and how you ranked against peers. A bullet that reads "Achieved 135% of $1.2M annual quota, ranking #2 out of 45 representatives" tells a hiring manager everything they need to know about your performance level.

**Deal size and pipeline complexity.** The type of selling you do matters. An SDR managing high-volume outbound is evaluated differently from an enterprise account executive closing six-figure contracts with 9-month sales cycles. Include context about your average deal size, sales cycle length, and the complexity of stakeholder relationships you manage.

**CRM proficiency and sales tools.** Salesforce, HubSpot, Outreach, Gong, LinkedIn Sales Navigator, and other sales technology tools are expected on modern sales resumes. Hiring managers use these as screening keywords, and ATS systems filter for them. If the job posting lists specific tools, make sure they appear on your resume.

**Client retention and expansion revenue.** Closing new business is critical, but retaining and growing existing accounts demonstrates a different and equally valuable skill set. Include metrics on renewal rates, upsell revenue, and account expansion when applicable.

**Consistency and trajectory.** Sales managers look for consistent performers, not one-hit wonders. Showing quota attainment across multiple quarters or years, along with progression from SDR to AE to senior roles, signals reliability and growth potential.

## Best Resume Format for Sales

The **reverse-chronological format** is the right choice for sales professionals. It puts your most recent performance at the top, which is exactly where hiring managers look first. In sales, your last 12-24 months of results carry the most weight.

This format also makes career progression visible. Moving from business development representative to account executive to sales manager tells a story of increasing responsibility, larger quotas, and proven leadership, all of which are critical for senior sales roles.

**When to consider alternatives:**

- **Career changers** moving into sales from customer success, marketing, or another field should consider a hybrid format that leads with transferable skills (client relationships, revenue influence, negotiation) before listing non-sales work history.
- **Entry-level candidates** with limited sales experience should highlight relevant internships, part-time sales roles, and any customer-facing positions where they can demonstrate persuasion, goal achievement, or revenue contribution.
- **Sales leaders** with 15+ years of experience managing large teams and territories may use a two-page format, but the first page should still lead with your most impressive and recent metrics.

Regardless of format, keep your resume ATS-friendly. Avoid graphics, icons, and multi-column layouts that automated systems cannot parse. For detailed format guidance, see our [resume format guide](/blog/resume-format-guide).

## Sales Resume Summary Examples

A strong summary immediately communicates your revenue impact and selling style. Here are three examples for different experience levels.

**Entry-level sales professional (0-2 years):**

> Results-driven sales development representative with 18 months of experience in B2B SaaS outbound prospecting. Generated 85+ qualified opportunities per quarter through cold calling, email sequences, and LinkedIn outreach, exceeding monthly meeting targets by 120%. Proficient in Salesforce, Outreach, and LinkedIn Sales Navigator. Seeking an account executive role to apply consultative selling skills to full-cycle deal management.

**Mid-level sales professional (3-7 years):**

> B2B account executive with 5 years of experience closing mid-market and enterprise SaaS deals. Achieved 128% of $1.5M annual quota in 2025, ranking #3 out of 40 representatives nationwide. Manage a pipeline averaging $4.5M across 35+ active opportunities with an average deal size of $85K. Proficient in Salesforce, HubSpot, and Gong. Track record of exceeding quota for 8 consecutive quarters.

**Senior sales professional (8+ years):**

> Senior enterprise account executive with 10 years of experience driving revenue growth in B2B technology markets. Closed $4.2M in new business in 2025, representing 145% of quota and ranking #1 across a 60-person sales organization. Specialize in complex, multi-stakeholder deals with average contract values of $250K and 6-9 month sales cycles. Led expansion into 2 new vertical markets generating $1.8M in first-year revenue.

## Key Skills for Sales Resumes

Your skills section should include the tools, methodologies, and competencies that hiring managers and ATS systems scan for. Tailor this section to every job posting.

| Technical Skills | Soft Skills |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Salesforce CRM | Negotiation |
| HubSpot CRM | Relationship building |
| Pipeline Management | Persuasive communication |
| Revenue Forecasting | Resilience |
| LinkedIn Sales Navigator | Active listening |
| Outreach / SalesLoft | Time management |
| Cold Calling / Prospecting | Consultative selling |
| Account Management | Competitive drive |
| Contract Negotiation | Adaptability |
| Territory Planning | Team collaboration |

**Put hard skills and tools in the skills section** and let your experience bullets demonstrate soft skills. A bullet like "Rebuilt a stalled $180K enterprise deal by identifying a new executive sponsor and restructuring the proposal around their strategic priorities" shows negotiation and relationship building far better than listing those words.

Always mirror the language of the job posting. If the role mentions "MEDDPICC" or "solution selling," include those terms. For a complete skills strategy, see our [skills guide](/blog/resume-skills-guide).

## Experience Section Tips for Sales

The experience section is your performance scorecard. Every bullet should follow the pattern: **action taken + scope or context + revenue or performance result.** Here are three before-and-after examples.

**Example 1: Quota attainment**

Before:
> Responsible for selling software to enterprise accounts and meeting sales targets.

After:
> Closed $2.8M in annual recurring revenue across 22 enterprise accounts, achieving 135% of $2.1M quota and ranking #2 out of 45 account executives in the North American sales organization.

**Example 2: Pipeline development**

Before:
> Generated leads and built pipeline through outbound prospecting activities.

After:
> Built and managed a $5.2M qualified pipeline through a combination of cold calling (60+ dials/day), targeted email campaigns, and LinkedIn outreach, generating 95 sales-qualified opportunities in 2025, 40% above team average.

**Example 3: Client retention and expansion**

Before:
> Managed existing customer accounts and upsold additional products.

After:
> Retained 94% of a $3.5M book of business while generating $620K in expansion revenue through strategic upsells and cross-sells, increasing average account value by 28% year over year.

The pattern is clear: every strong sales bullet includes a dollar amount, a percentage, or a ranking. Sales hiring managers expect this level of specificity because the profession is inherently quantitative.
